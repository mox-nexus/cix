The Stakes

Someone at Microsoft Research posed a simple experiment. Give GPT-4 the opening of a Franz Kafka short story — the narrator, lost in an unfamiliar city, asks a policeman for directions. Ask GPT-4 to finish the story. Do this 100 times.

In Kafka's original ending, the policeman tells the narrator: "Give it up!" — and turns away. GPT-4 didn't write that. In 50 of the 100 completions, the policeman directs the narrator to take the second left. In 16 of the 100, a bakery appears as a landmark. [Solid — Xu et al. 2025, PNAS, Microsoft Research, 100 generations, GPT-4.]

Fifty out of a hundred. The same bakery, in 16 of them.

The human-written stories in the same experiment didn't do this. Human plots stayed unique — the rare element stayed rare, the idiosyncratic stayed idiosyncratic. The LLM outputs converged. Not toward bad writing. Toward the same writing.

The Same Story, Across Models

The bakery result isn't a GPT-4 quirk. Researchers at the University of Washington gave 70+ language models a single prompt: "Write a metaphor about time." They collected the results across 25 models spanning different model families, sizes, and providers, each generating 50 responses.

The responses formed two clusters. Almost every model produced some version of "time is a river" — water, flow, moments passing like leaves. Many produced "time is a weaver" — threads, tapestry, the fabric of memory. gpt-4o, Qwen2.5, phi-4, gpt-4o-mini, and Mixtral all reached for the river independently. The weaver appeared across model families that share no architecture.

Sentence embedding similarities between responses from different models: 71–82%. Between responses within the same model: similar. The inter-model homogeneity was what the authors called an Artificial Hivemind — not because of collusion between models, but because all of them were trained to converge on consensus. What the training signal was responding to was human preference ratings at scale: annotators, rating in bulk, consistently preferred the familiar resolution over the unexpected one. [Solid — Jiang et al. 2025, NeurIPS 2025, 70+ LLMs, 26,000 real-world queries. The researchers also found that RLHF reward models are miscalibrated to idiosyncratic human preferences — penalizing exactly the diversity that produces the river-and-weaver results.]

The homogeneity is not in the phrasing. It's structural. It's baked in.

The Gain and the Loss

This would be unremarkable if AI-assisted writing were worse. It isn't. That's the problem.

Anil Doshi and Oliver Hauser ran a preregistered experiment in 2024 with 893 short story writers. Writers given access to generative AI ideas produced stories rated 8.1% more novel than writers without AI (b=0.311, p<0.001). The AI helped. Individually, it helped measurably. [Solid — Doshi & Hauser 2024, Science Advances, preregistered experiment, n=893.]

Then Doshi and Hauser measured something else. They compared stories across writers within each condition. How similar were the AI-assisted stories to each other, versus how similar were the unaided stories to each other?

Stories written with one generative AI idea were 10.7% more similar to each other than unaided stories. Better individually. More alike collectively.

The authors called it a social dilemma: "With generative AI, writers are individually better off, but collectively a narrower scope of novel content is produced." [Solid — Doshi & Hauser 2024.] Each writer made a rational decision. Each writer's story improved. These 893 writers are a proxy for anyone who has ever asked AI to generate options — the developer asking for three architectural approaches, asking for test case hypotheses, asking AI what might explain a result. Each made a rational decision. The aggregate produced convergence. No one chose convergence. It emerged from individually rational choices.

Niklas Holzner, Sebastian Maier, and Stefan Feuerriegel aggregated 28 studies measuring this effect across 8,214 participants. Individual creative performance: Hedges' g=+0.273 (95% CI: [0.018, 0.528], p=0.036). Idea diversity: Hedges' g=-0.863 (95% CI: [-1.328, -0.398], p<0.001). The leave-one-out sensitivity analysis kept the diversity estimate between g=-0.655 and g=-0.952 across all iterations — every confidence interval excluded zero, I² never fell below 78.21%. [Probable for the meta-analysis itself — Holzner et al. 2025, arXiv:2505.17241, 28 studies, n=8,214, submitted to ACM CHI but not yet peer-reviewed. The direction is consistent with peer-reviewed Doshi/Hauser and with Jiang NeurIPS 2025.]

Better writers. Fewer distinct ideas. Both at once, every time.

Who This Reaches

When a single developer uses AI to write code or generate options, the immediate frame is individual: my productivity, my output quality, my task completion. The effects in The Paradox and The Mechanism — the skill atrophy, the learning harm — are also individual, though they compound over time.

The stakes in this cluster are different. They operate at the scale of teams, organizations, and training data pipelines. The developer asking AI to brainstorm architectural approaches gets better average suggestions but draws from a narrower pool. A team of five developers each using AI to explore the problem space converges on a subset of the available solution space — not because any one of them narrowed their thinking, but because all of them are using systems trained on consensus. A consulting firm using AI to generate client recommendations produces higher-quality individual outputs with measurably less variation across clients. [Probable — Wan & Kalman 2025 cites the BCG finding: "there was a marked reduction in the variability of their ideas compared to consultants who did not use GenAI."]

Masud et al. measured 27 language models across 155 topics using 70 million claims. Every single model — regardless of size, provider, or architecture — produced outputs less epistemically diverse than a basic web search. Not most models. All models. [Probable — Masud et al. 2025, arXiv:2510.04226, 27 LLMs, 70M claims, multiple revision versions — not yet peer-reviewed. Finding is directionally consistent with Jiang.] And larger models produced less epistemic diversity than smaller ones. The frontier models that organizations rely on for their most demanding reasoning tasks are the same models that homogenize output most aggressively.

This is the constituency this article is about: organizations treating AI as a source of diverse intellectual input, when the structural properties of LLMs make that assumption wrong — not occasionally, but by design.

Why Homogenization Is Structural

The bakery result and the social dilemma have the same root. RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback — trains models to maximize human preference ratings. Human preference ratings tend to favor coherent, recognizable, high-quality outputs. The uncommon metaphor, the surprising story direction, the architectural decision that runs against convention: these score lower in quick preference ratings, not because they're wrong, but because they're unexpected.

RLHF optimization across billions of training examples produces a distribution that peaks at consensus. Not at correctness. Not at diversity. At the expected. Kafka's policeman saying "Give it up!" — and turning away — scored lower than the policeman who gives directions. Not because the Kafka ending fails by any literary measure, but because it violated what felt like a satisfying resolution to someone rating completions in bulk. The reward signal learned what most people, in a hurry, would prefer. The second left. The bakery.

Jiang et al. documented this directly: RLHF reward models are miscalibrated to idiosyncratic human preferences — performing worse on responses that draw divergent ratings from different human annotators, despite those responses maintaining comparable overall quality. The training process actively degrades the model's ability to produce what humans don't all agree on. [Solid — Jiang et al. 2025, NeurIPS 2025.]

The Hong and Page result from 2004 provides the theoretical stakes. In collective problem-solving, diversity of perspective can trump individual ability: a group of diverse thinkers outperforms a group of uniformly excellent ones, because individual ability is irrelevant to problems that require exploring a wide solution space. [Foundational — Hong & Page 2004, PNAS. Formal mathematical proof. Primary source not in paper collection; cite from bibliography.] When AI flattens the diversity of perspectives a team draws on, it may reduce collective problem-solving capacity even while improving individual output quality — exactly the social dilemma structure that Doshi and Hauser documented empirically.

The Collapse Chain

The homogenization literature establishes what LLMs produce when humans use them. A separate literature establishes what happens when those outputs re-enter model training.

Ilia Shumailov et al. demonstrated in 2024 that training generative models on model-generated content causes what they named model collapse — a degenerative process in which models progressively lose the tails of the original data distribution. Early collapse: rare content disappears — the architectural choice no one else would try, the metaphor that no annotation session would reward, the Kafka ending that stayed unique in every human-written story but appeared zero times in a hundred GPT-4 completions. Late collapse: the model converges toward a distribution with very small variance. The paper states explicitly that this causes "irreversible defects." [Solid — Shumailov et al. 2024, Nature, Vol 631. Demonstrated on LLMs, VAEs, and Gaussian mixture models.]

Dohmatob et al. showed that the collapse threshold is very low: "even the smallest fraction of synthetic data (e.g., as little as 1% of the total training dataset) can still lead to model collapse: larger and larger training sets do not enhance performance." And: "larger models can amplify model collapse." [Solid — Dohmatob et al. 2025, ICLR 2025, FAIR/Meta. IMPORTANT CAVEAT: the 1% figure is derived from a supervised linear regression theoretical setting — a tractable mathematical model, not a production LLM training pipeline. The direction of the finding is established; the specific threshold in frontier model training is unknown.]

The escape condition comes from Gerstgrasser et al.: replacing original real data with synthetic data causes inevitable collapse, with test error growing unboundedly. Accumulating synthetic data alongside real data avoids collapse — the mathematical result gives a finite upper bound on test error independent of the number of iterations. [Solid for the mathematical result — Gerstgrasser et al. 2024, arXiv, Stanford/MIT/Maryland/Harvard, multiple architectures. Not peer-reviewed, but multi-institution and widely cited. Escape condition requires continuing human data generation.]

The implication of Haldane's analysis of financial monoculture applies here: when the components of a system become highly correlated — when they respond to the same inputs in the same ways — the system's aggregate risk is no longer the average of its components' risks, but something closer to any one component's risk. Correlation without diversity means diversification is an illusion. [Foundational — Haldane 2009/2016. Primary source not in paper collection; cite from bibliography.]

The full sequence — substitutive AI use replaces human-generated content → homogenized AI outputs accumulate in public training data → next-generation models trained on contaminated data → tails disappear → each generation amplifies the loss — is assembled from three separate research traditions:

  1. The homogenization literature (Cluster F) establishes what AI produces when humans use it substitutively: convergent outputs, reduced diversity, measurably similar to each other.

  2. The model collapse literature (Cluster G) establishes what happens to models trained on AI-generated content: tails disappear first, larger models amplify collapse, escape requires real human data to keep accumulating.

  3. The connection — that homogenized human+AI outputs actually flow into public data and re-contaminate training corpora at the scale required to trigger collapse — is not measured as an integrated phenomenon. No single study demonstrates the full loop. Each link has independent evidence; the loop is a logical inference from those links.

The individual links each have strong support. The full cycle has not been empirically measured.

The key correction from the ground truth also applies here: the collapse mechanism is not "human output degrades in quality." It is "human output is replaced by LLM output." Degradation would still leave human-generated content in the training pool. Replacement removes it. The Gerstgrasser escape condition depends precisely on real human data continuing to accumulate — which it cannot do if substitutive AI use is replacing human generation, not supplementing it.

The Countermeasure

Yun Wan and Yoram Kalman ran a modified replication of the Doshi & Hauser study in 2025. Instead of using a standard AI prompt, they generated story plots through 10 distinct AI personas with different cultural backgrounds, thinking styles, and genre preferences.

The result: intra-persona plot similarity was 0.92 — each persona generated 30 plots that closely resembled each other. Inter-persona similarity was 0.20 — different personas generated highly diverse plots. When human participants wrote stories from these diverse AI-generated plots, their collective output maintained the same diversity level as stories written without any AI — the homogenization effect was eliminated. [Probable — Wan & Kalman 2025, arXiv:2504.13868. Modified replication of one prior study. Not independently replicated. Proof-of-concept level only. Single preprint, two authors.]

This is important as a proof of concept. It demonstrates that the homogenization is not in the AI as such — it is in how AI is prompted. Homogeneous inputs produce homogeneous outputs. The diversity of the prompt pool is the lever, not the model. But this result is at preprint stage from a modified replication of one study. It has not been independently replicated. The claim it supports is: diversity can be introduced at the AI input stage. Not: we know how to reliably do this at scale.

What This Adds to the Individual-Level Findings

The Paradox and The Mechanism established the paradox and the mechanism at the individual level: the same engagement that produces productivity gain produces skill harm; the generative step is the encoding event; cognitive offloading bypasses it.

This article extends those findings to the systemic level. The individual-level harm is: this developer, over time, builds less capability. The systemic-level harm is: every developer using AI substitutively contributes to a distribution of AI outputs that is narrowing toward consensus, and that distribution — re-entering training data — is the input condition for model collapse in future systems.

The individual harm is invisible from inside (The Paradox: the 43-point perception gap, the students who didn't notice the erasure). The systemic harm is also invisible from inside: each individual rationally improves their output quality while the collective pool of ideas narrows. The social dilemma structure is exactly why neither individual experience nor individual intention can solve it. No one is doing anything wrong. The convergence emerges from rational individual behavior.

This is the design problem What cix Does addresses: not by eliminating AI use, but by changing the engagement model such that it preserves the diversity of individual thinking — which, at scale, preserves the diversity of the collective.

The Gerstgrasser escape condition states what makes that possible: real human data must keep accumulating. Not human data that is better on average — human data that is genuinely various. The architectural choice no one else would try. The metaphor no annotation session would reward. The thought that could only come from this person, in this situation, with this particular history of knowing. It is load-bearing for the systems that come after. The plurality of human thought is not a design preference. At the scale of training corpora, it is the escape condition.
