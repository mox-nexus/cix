## Source landscape gaps to probe during collection

Topics that fall inside scope but may not be well-covered by the tradition entry points above. Recon should actively search for these.

- **Memory for conversation specifically** — sub-literature on dialogue memory, conversational pragmatics, and recall of discursive material. May exist under "discourse memory" or "conversational memory" keywords.
- **Insight in real-world conversational settings** — most insight research is laboratory problem-solving. Whether lab findings transfer to the kind of discursive insight Yash describes is itself a research question. Look for ecological validity studies.
- **Phenomenology × cognitive neuroscience hybrids** — work that explicitly tries to triangulate first-person and third-person evidence on memory. Less common but high value. Possibly Francisco Varela's neurophenomenology.
- **Semiotics × cognitive psychology hybrids** — work bridging Peircean signs to empirical cognitive science. Deacon is the obvious entry; there may be others.
- **"Fog match" as a concept** — the user's phrase for how recall cues match partial states. This may not be called "fog match" anywhere; could be context-dependent memory, state-dependent memory, or something more specific under a different name.

## Excluded sources

Explicitly will not cite:

- Pop neuroscience trade books with low replication standards (Norman Doidge, Jonah Lehrer)
- Self-help memory training guides (Joshua Foer *Moonwalking with Einstein* is borderline — include only as journalism, not evidence)
- Single-study clickbait psychology
- Unreplicated 1990s priming studies (the priming literature has serious replication problems)
- Content marketing disguised as thought leadership
