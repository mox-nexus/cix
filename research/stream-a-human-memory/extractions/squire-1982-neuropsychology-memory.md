# Extraction: Squire 1982 — Neuropsychology of Human Memory

## Atomic Claims

### squire1982:c1
- **Claim:** Global amnesia selectively impairs acquisition of new memories and recall of some premorbid memories, while sparing intellectual capacity, digit span, social skills, and early-life knowledge.
- **Quote:** "The globally amnesic patient can appear normal to casual observation. Such a patient may have normal intellectual capacity, normal digit span and intact social skills... The defect lies in acquiring new memories and in recalling some memories that had been acquired prior to becoming amnesic."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c2
- **Claim:** Patient H.M.'s bilateral medial temporal resection (hippocampal formation, parahippocampal gyrus, amygdala, uncus) produced profound amnesia with no detectable change in general intellectual ability.
- **Quote:** "H. M. sustained bilateral resection of the medial temporal region... Following surgery, H. M. exhibited a profound amnesic syndrome in the absence of any detectable change in general intellectual ability."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c3
- **Claim:** Diencephalic amnesia (Korsakoff, N.A.) exhibits normal forgetting rate; bitemporal amnesia (H.M.) and ECT amnesia exhibit abnormally rapid forgetting — two distinct syndromes.
- **Quote:** "diencephalic amnesia... is characterized by a normal rate of forgetting. By contrast, bitemporal amnesia... and the amnesia associated with ECT are characterized by rapid forgetting."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c4
- **Claim:** Cueing evidence does not support the hypothesis that amnesia is a retrieval failure for adequately stored information.
- **Quote:** "the results with cues of various types provide no basis for supposing that amnesia reflects a failure to retrieve information that is adequately stored."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ3

### squire1982:c5
- **Claim:** The memory performance pattern in diencephalic amnesia mirrors normal forgetting, suggesting these amnesias produce weak memory representations rather than retrieval deficits.
- **Quote:** "the pattern of performance exhibited by amnesic patients... was recapitulated by normal subjects during the course of normal forgetting."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ2, RQ3

### squire1982:c6
- **Claim:** Permanent long-term memory storage occurs outside the medial temporal and diencephalic regions; those regions serve the formation and post-learning maintenance of new memories.
- **Quote:** "permanent memory storage normally occurs outside the brain regions affected in amnesia. It appears that these brain regions constitute an essential neuroanatomical substrate for the formation of new memories and for their maintenance and elaboration after learning."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ2
- **Note:** Storage is neocortical; hippocampus is for formation/consolidation, not long-term storage. Directly relevant to the "where is the memory" question.

### squire1982:c7
- **Claim:** The medial temporal region's consolidation role can persist for up to several years after initial learning.
- **Quote:** "the medial temporal region plays a role in the development and consolidation of memory and that this role can continue for up to a few years after initial learning."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0, RQ2
- **Note:** This is the classical consolidation framework that Nader & Hardt 2009 reconsolidation challenges.

### squire1982:c8
- **Claim:** Temporally limited retrograde amnesia (affecting 1-2 years) is consistent with Ribot's Law and implies memories become progressively more resistant to disruption with time.
- **Quote:** "Memory can be lost for events of the past one to two years without affecting more remote memories... This finding also implies a normal process by which memory gradually becomes more resistant to disruption with the passage of time."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0, RQ2

### squire1982:c9
- **Claim:** Patient H.M. demonstrated progressive motor learning (mirror-tracing) over three days despite having no conscious memory of prior practice — dissociating skill acquisition from declarative memory.
- **Quote:** "H. M. exhibited progressive learning of a mirror-tracing task across three days of testing, despite reporting on each day that he had no memory of having performed the task before."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c10
- **Claim:** Amnesic patients acquired mirror-reading skill at a normal rate and retained it at three months, despite no memory for the testing situation or words read.
- **Quote:** "The amnesic patients improved their skill at this task at a normal rate over a period of three days and retained the skill at a normal level three months later."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c11
- **Claim:** Amnesic patients can acquire procedural knowledge (rules/skills) normally but cannot retain declarative knowledge (specific items/data) — establishing the declarative/procedural dissociation.
- **Quote:** "amnesic patients can learn the procedures needed for acquisition and retention of mirror-reading skills, but cannot remember the specific data, i.e. the words"
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c12
- **Claim:** Declarative knowledge and procedural knowledge depend on fundamentally different kinds of neurological organization.
- **Quote:** "The evidence now suggests that such a distinction is honored by the nervous system, and that these two domains of information depend on fundamentally different kinds of neurological organization."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0
- **Note:** THE foundational taxonomy. Memex operates in the declarative domain.

### squire1982:c13
- **Claim:** Declarative memory (facts, faces, words, events) requires intact bitemporal and diencephalic structures; procedural memory (motor skills, mirror-reading) does not.
- **Quote:** "the ability to develop and store declarative memory... depends on the integrity of the particular bitemporal and diencephalic brain structures affected in amnesia."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c14
- **Claim:** Procedural knowledge is implicit by nature: accessible only through engaging in the procedures, not through explicit recall.
- **Quote:** "procedural knowledge is considered by its nature to be implicit — accessible only by engaging in or applying the procedures in which the knowledge is contained."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c15
- **Claim:** There is no psychological evidence requiring the hypothesis that all memory storage is permanent.
- **Quote:** "there are no psychological data that would require such an idea."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ2

### squire1982:c16
- **Claim:** Specific episodic memories can dissipate from storage while still influencing behavior through incorporation into generic schemas.
- **Quote:** "Memory of a particular face, word, or event could dissipate from storage but could nevertheless influence behavior in a less literal, but long-lasting way by virtue of having been incorporated into more generic representations or schemata"
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ2
- **Note:** Directly connects Bartlett's schema theory to the neuropsychological evidence. The "durable unit" is the schema, not the specific episode.

### squire1982:c17
- **Claim:** An experience might leave no trace aiding later recognition yet tune neural mechanisms (schemas) specialized for the relevant domain.
- **Quote:** "An experience of a particular face might leave no neural trace that would aid later recognition of the same face; yet it could tune or elaborate neural mechanisms (schemata) specialized for facial recognition."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ2, RQ3

### squire1982:c18
- **Claim:** H.M. performed normally on premorbid famous-faces recognition (1920-1950) but recognized fewer than 20% of faces from after his 1953 surgery.
- **Quote:** "H. M. performed as well as control subjects in recognizing famous faces from his premorbid period, 1920-1950, but identified fewer than 20% of the faces of individuals who became prominent after his surgery in 1953"
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c19
- **Claim:** Squire argues the prior unitary model of amnesia (single system, single store) is inadequate; evidence supports two distinct disorders corresponding to diencephalic vs. medial temporal damage.
- **Quote:** "The neuropsychological and anatomical facts now tell a richer story in which amnesia may reflect either of two disorders corresponding to damage in the diencephalic or the medial temporal regions."
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0

### squire1982:c20
- **Claim:** The amnesic deficit is not selective for episodic memory alone; amnesic patients are equally impaired at acquiring new episodic and new semantic information.
- **Quote:** "Amnesic patients have seemingly equal difficulty acquiring both new episodic information... and semantic information"
- **Tier:** 2 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ0
- **Note:** Challenges the Tulving episodic/semantic distinction as the relevant boundary for amnesia. The neuropsychological cut is declarative/procedural, not episodic/semantic.

## Extraction Notes

- Full-text extraction from Annual Review of Neuroscience. 38 claims in raw agent output; condensed to 20 highest-impact.
- c12 (declarative/procedural dissociation) is THE foundational RQ0 taxonomy. Memex operates in the declarative domain — everything memex stores (conversations, documents, insights) is declarative by nature.
- c16-c17 (schema persistence after episode dissipation) bridges Bartlett and neuropsychology: the specific memory fades but shapes schemas that persist. This is a mechanism for Yash's "idea solidification" — the specific conversation is forgotten, but the schema it shaped remains.
- c20 challenges the Tulving extraction's reliance on the episodic/semantic boundary. Squire argues the neuropsychologically real boundary is declarative/procedural, not episodic/semantic. Both could be true — one is a functional distinction (Tulving), the other a neuroanatomical one (Squire). Synthesis should address whether they're compatible.
- The classical consolidation framework in c7 is directly challenged by Nader & Hardt 2009 c5 (reconsolidation). Squire says consolidation is a one-time process lasting up to years; Nader says it re-opens on every retrieval. This is the central tension for RQ0.
