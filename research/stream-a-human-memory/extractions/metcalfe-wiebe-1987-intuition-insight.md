# Extraction: Metcalfe & Wiebe 1987 — Intuition in Insight

## Atomic Claims

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c1
- **Claim:** Warmth ratings (feeling of approaching solution) show a more incremental pattern for algebra and noninsight problems than for insight problems.
- **Quote:** "the patterns-of-warmth ratings, which reflect subjects' feelings of approaching solution, differed for insight and noninsight problems."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1, RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c2
- **Claim:** The subjective phenomenology of insight problem solution is characterized by suddenness and lack of foreshadowing.
- **Quote:** "the phenomenology of insight-problem solution was characterized by a sudden, unforeseen flash of illumination."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c3
- **Claim:** Metcalfe and Wiebe propose that the empirically demonstrated phenomenological difference (sudden vs. incremental warmth) should serve as the operational definition of insight.
- **Quote:** "We propose that the difference in phenomenology accompanying insight and noninsight problem solving, as empirically demonstrated here, be used to define insight."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c4
- **Claim:** Pre-solution feeling-of-knowing ratings predicted performance on algebra problems but not on insight problems.
- **Quote:** "subjective feeling of knowing predicted performance on algebra problems but not on insight problems"
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c5
- **Claim:** Subjects systematically overestimated how many problems they would solve, with greater overestimation for insight problems.
- **Quote:** "subjects' expectations of performance greatly exceeded their actual performance, especially on insight problems"
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c6
- **Claim:** Normative difficulty rankings predicted individual problem-solving performance better than subjects' own FOK predictions, especially for insight problems.
- **Quote:** "normative predictions provided a better estimate of individual performance than did subjects' own predictions, especially on the insight problems"
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c7
- **Claim:** In Metcalfe 1986b, warmth ratings remained low and flat until solution on insight problems, then jumped — the step-function pattern.
- **Quote:** "If the problems were solved by what subjectively is a sudden flash of insight, one would expect that the warmth ratings would be fairly low and constant until solution, at which point they would jump to a high value. This is what was found."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1
- **Note:** This is the BEST empirical evidence for the "click is real" question. Step-function warmth pattern = measurable signature of sudden insight.

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c8
- **Claim:** On 78% of correctly solved insight problems, warmth ratings rose by no more than 1 point on a 10-point scale across the entire solution interval.
- **Quote:** "On 78% of the problems and anagrams for which subjects provided the correct solution, the progress estimates increased by no more than 1 point, on a 10-point scale, over the entire solution interval."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c9
- **Claim:** On insight problems where subjects gave incorrect answers, warmth showed an incremental pattern (unlike the flat-then-jump for correct solutions).
- **Quote:** "On those problems for which the wrong answer was given, however, the warmth protocols showed a more incremental pattern."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1, RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c10
- **Claim:** In Experiment 1, the gamma correlation between problem type and incremental warmth was positive and significant (angular G=.26, differential G=.23), confirming insight problems have less incremental warmth.
- **Quote:** "The overall correlation on the angular warmth measure was .26, which is significantly different from zero [t(18) = 2.02]."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1, RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c11
- **Claim:** In Experiment 2, insight problems showed significantly less incremental warmth slopes than algebra problems (angular G=.35, differential G=.32).
- **Quote:** "Gammas computed on the angular warmth measures indicated that the insight problems showed a less incremental slope than did the algebra problems: Mean G = .35, which is significantly greater than zero."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1, RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c12
- **Claim:** In Experiment 2, FOK-performance gamma was significantly higher for algebra (.40) than insight (.08); algebra FOK was significantly above zero, insight FOK was not.
- **Quote:** "There was a significant difference in gamma between the algebra problems (mean G = .40) and the insight problems (mean G = .08) [F(1,46) = 6.46]."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c13
- **Claim:** Subjects predicted solving 59% of insight problems but actually solved 34%; overestimation was significantly greater for insight than algebra.
- **Quote:** "Predicted performance on the insight problems was .59, whereas actual performance was only .34."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c14
- **Claim:** Normative predictions outperformed subjects' own FOK predictions significantly more for insight than algebra problems (interaction F(1,46)=10.13), disconfirming privileged self-knowledge for insight.
- **Quote:** "The idea that subjects may have privileged access to idiosyncratic information that makes them especially able to predict their own performance was overwhelmingly wrong in this experiment."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4
- **Note:** LOAD-BEARING for memex: people do NOT have privileged metacognitive access to their own insight processes.

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c15
- **Claim:** The zero FOK correlation for insight cannot be explained by restricted difficulty range, because normative predictions on the same problems achieved G=.77.
- **Quote:** "the zero feeling-of-knowing correlation, discussed earlier, is therefore probably not attributable to a restricted range of insight-problem difficulty."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ4

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c16
- **Claim:** The authors propose using step-function warmth protocols as a diagnostic criterion to operationally define insight at the individual-problem and individual-subject level.
- **Quote:** "If we find problems (or indeed problems for particular individuals) that are accompanied by step-function warmth protocols during the solution interval, we may define those problems as being insight problems for those people."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1
- **Note:** This is the proposal for a DETECTABLE signature of insight — relevant to whether memex could detect clicks.

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c17
- **Claim:** Metcalfe and Wiebe propose defining insight in terms of antecedent phenomenology captured by real-time metacognitive monitoring, not by problem category or structural features.
- **Quote:** "We propose that insight be defined in terms of the antecedent phenomenology that may be monitored by metacognitive assessments by the subject."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1

### metcalfe-wiebe-1987:c18
- **Claim:** Across both experiments, subjective metacognitions predicted noninsight/algebra performance but failed to predict insight performance.
- **Quote:** "people's subjective metacognitions were predictive of performance on the noninsight problems, but not on the insight problems."
- **Tier:** 1 | **Confidence:** High | **RQ:** RQ1, RQ4

## Extraction Notes

- Full-text extraction. The companion paper to Metcalfe 1986 (already extracted from abstract). This paper adds the WARMTH RATING methodology — continuous real-time metacognitive monitoring during problem solving, not just pre-solution FOK. This is stronger evidence because it captures the temporal dynamics, not just a single prediction.
- c7-c8 together form the single strongest empirical evidence that insight IS a sudden event: 78% of correct insight solutions showed ≤1 point warmth change on a 10-point scale across the ENTIRE solving interval, then jumped at solution.
- c9 adds a critical nuance: incorrect "insight" attempts DO show incremental warmth — suggesting that when insight fails, the solver is actually doing something more like gradual search (which produces warmth rise). True insight produces no warmth rise.
- c14 is architecturally critical for memex: the hypothesis that users have "privileged self-knowledge" about their own insight processes is "overwhelmingly wrong." Memex should not rely on user self-report of insight quality.
